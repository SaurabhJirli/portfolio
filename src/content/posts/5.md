At work, we collect data from IoT devices — sensors reporting temperature, humidity, power metrics, and other readings. The protocol we use for this is MQTT, and it's been a solid fit.

## What MQTT is

MQTT is a lightweight messaging protocol designed for constrained devices and low-bandwidth networks. It follows a publish/subscribe model — devices publish messages to topics, and consumers subscribe to those topics.

## Why it works for IoT

- **Small overhead** — MQTT packets are tiny compared to HTTP, which matters when devices are battery-powered or on flaky networks
- **Persistent sessions** — if a device disconnects and reconnects, it can pick up missed messages
- **QoS levels** — you can choose between at-most-once, at-least-once, and exactly-once delivery depending on how critical the data is

## Our setup

1. Devices publish sensor readings to an **Eclipse Mosquitto** broker
2. A Spring Boot consumer subscribes to relevant topics
3. Incoming data is validated, transformed, and written to **InfluxDB**
4. A separate service reads from InfluxDB and serves dashboard queries

## Gotchas

- **Topic design matters** — we use a hierarchical structure like `devices/{device_id}/sensors/{sensor_type}` which makes it easy to subscribe to specific devices or sensor types
- **QoS 2 is slow** — we use QoS 1 for most things and handle duplicates at the application layer
- **Mosquitto needs tuning** for anything beyond small deployments — max connections, message size limits, and persistence settings all need adjusting

## The takeaway

MQTT is simple, well-supported, and battle-tested for IoT. If you're collecting data from hardware devices, it's worth learning.
