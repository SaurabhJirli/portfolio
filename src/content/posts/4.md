Microservices were the first architecture I worked with professionally. I'd read about them in blogs and thought I understood the idea. Then I actually had to build and maintain them. Here's what I got wrong early on.

## Mistake 1: Too many services too early

It's tempting to split everything into its own service. But every new service means another repo, another deployment, another thing to monitor. For a small team, fewer well-structured services beat a dozen tiny ones.

## Mistake 2: Ignoring service boundaries

I kept creating services that needed to call each other synchronously. That's basically a distributed monolith with extra network hops. The real value comes when services can operate independently.

## Mistake 3: Not thinking about failure

In a monolith, a function call either works or throws an exception. With microservices, the network is involved — and the network fails. I didn't think about retries, timeouts, or circuit breakers until things broke in production.

## Mistake 4: Shared databases

Two services sharing a database sounds convenient. Until one team's migration breaks another team's queries. Each service should own its data.

## What I'd tell myself a year ago

- Start with a monolith if you can, and extract services when the boundaries are clear
- Use async messaging (Kafka, etc.) between services whenever possible
- Invest in observability early — logs, traces, and metrics
- Read about the fallacies of distributed computing

Microservices are a tradeoff, not an upgrade. They solve some problems and create others.
